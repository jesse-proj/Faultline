The Trap of Raw Resource Extraction: Resource Rich, but Economically Disadvantaged

By Faultline Investigative Desk | March 2026

Nickel is a highly valued element as it is highly versatile. It has been used in energy storage technologies for years, and this is due to its ability to improve storage capacity at low cost, which was instrumental in miniaturizing batteries and enabling the portable electronic devices that we use every single day. It is also a key material in the cathodes of lithium-ion batteries used for electric vehicles or EVs. It is estimated that the demand for nickel would grow by as much as 40% by 2030 as bans on internal combustion engine vehicles approach in many markets (IEF, 2024).

However, why is the Philippines still poor, despite being a major contributor to the nickel market and also the second-largest producer of nickel ore globally?

"This is because they export raw ore at low prices instead of processing it into high-value heavy machinery or electronics domestically."

By exporting raw materials, the wealth generated by "value-adding" manufacturing stays in wealthy nations, leaving the source country with the environmental costs but none of the industrial profit.

The country is one example of many in the Global South struggling to prosper due to the heavy dependence on raw exports. It is ironic that even with the abundant natural riches that the Philippines has, it is in a far worse economic situation than other countries that are far less endowed than it (Habito, 2022).

Simply relying on natural resources is not enough; what is valued in today's time is the finished product that's manufactured from these materials because it is what the consumers actually buy.

Although in spite of this, many countries face the "tariff escalation" wherein processed or finished goods are taxed more than the raw materials that are used to make them (UNCTAD, 2025).

So, even though raw material is essentially worthless unless it is processed, the system itself hinders countries from developing. This is also why many resource-rich countries choose to sell raw materials, as it is cheaper.

These countries fail to fully use the maximum value of their resources due to the different barriers in the modern world that prevent them from growing. This shows a larger systemic issue at hand, and how there needs to be reforms done in the global trade, which highly favors the wealthier countries.

Labor Exploitation and Wealth Gap

By Faultline Investigative Desk | March 2026

Globalization has allowed the globe to connect in many different aspects of life. With such a positive description, it is unfortunate that the concept has brought forth cases of exploitation and inequality especially when discussing labor and wealth.

Despite globalization allowing for innovation, expanding the economy, and granting opportunities, in reality most of the generated profit is held by a small percentage of the population on the planet (Petersen, 2024).

Worldwide in 2024, 1.55 billion adults had a net worth of less than 10,000 U.S. dollars compared to 60 million adults that had a net worth of more than 1,000,000 U.S. dollars (Statista, 2026). These elite earners are roughly 0.04% of the sample size. Thus evidently, there is a massive disparity between wealth across the wealthy across the population.

One trend which is important to discuss is the competition between cheap labor. High-skilled workers do have the edge in obtaining opportunities, however low-skilled workers often face threat of competition from cheaper labor markets abroad (Petersen, 2024).

Low-skilled workers are in a constant state of pressure in an environment where gaining skills is already difficult when even children are exploited for labor, robbing them of education (Effects of economic globalization, n.d.).

This competition also affects local jobs in developed nations as companies would rather outsource labor for cheap. Additionally, companies often participate in exploitative behavior of their workers to further cut costs (Biscontini, 2025).

Between income disparity and an exploitative environment, much of the population are forced into borderline unlivable living conditions. 2.2 billion people do not have access to clean water, 750 million do not have electricity, 251 million are out of school, etc. (Global Poverty Today).

The Global Environment Crisis and Poverty

By Faultline Investigative Desk | March 2026

Developing nations face a continous cycle of disaster recovery, severely stunting long-term economic growth. A staggering new report reveals the devastating financial and human burden of cost of the climate crisis, proving that low-income nations are paying the highest price for global emissions.

According to the Germanwatch Climate Risk Index 2026, extreme weather events have caused more then over $4.5 trillion in cumulative economic losses globally between 1995 and 2024. However, the burden of these staggering losses is not shared equally across the globe.

The data highlights a glaring disparity: eight of the ten countries most affected by climate disasters in 2024 fall into the low or lower-middle-income brackets. Storms accounted for a massive all time high of 58% of the total economic damages, followed closely by catastrophic, widespread flooding that decimated local agriculture and housing.

"These vulnerable nations are caught in a relentless 'poverty trap,' entirely unable to financially recover from one climate disaster before the next one strikes."

This relentless and constant cycle makes long-term economic development almost impossible. When a severe typhoon or multi-week flood destroys regional infrastructure, cash crops, and homes, developing nations are forced to divert their scarce sovereign resources. Funding is pulled away from education, healthcare, and poverty reduction, and poured instead into immediate emergency recovery and debris removal project.

The Germanwatch findings underscore the urgent need for robust international climate finance mechanisms. Without substantial and immediate support from high-emitting, wealthier nations—specifically through functional Loss and Damage funds—the world's poorest populations will continue to bear the crushing weight of a climate crisis they did little to create.

Breaking the Cycle: How Ordinary People Can Help Fight Global Inequality

By Faultline Investigative Desk | March 2026

In today’s global economy, many developing countries face problems that keep them stuck in poverty. These include relying too much on exporting raw materials, unfair treatment of workers, and the growing effects of climate change. These issues may seem too big to solve, but ordinary citizens can still make a real difference through their everyday choices and actions.

One major problem is the trap of raw resource extraction. Many countries sell raw materials instead of turning them into finished products, which limits their income and growth. One simple way people can help is by supporting locally made goods. When consumers choose local products, they help strengthen local industries and reduce the need to rely on exporting raw materials. According to the Philippine News Agency, supporting local production can make the economy stronger, especially during global crises (Zaldarriaga, 2025). Buying local also encourages businesses to invest in better technology and create higher-value products. Over time, this creates jobs, supports small businesses, and builds a more stable economy.

Another issue is labor exploitation and the growing gap between the rich and the poor. Many workers are paid low wages while large companies earn most of the profit. Citizens can help by becoming more mindful of what they buy. Every purchase supports how a product was made and how workers were treated. By choosing companies that provide fair wages and safe working conditions, people can push businesses to improve their practices (Sustainability Directory, n.d.). At the same time, working together is important. Supporting unions, cooperatives, and worker rights groups helps give workers a stronger voice. The International Labour Organization explains that fair wages and collective bargaining are key to making sure workers are treated with respect and paid properly (International Labour Organization, n.d.). Through both personal choices and group action, citizens can help create a fairer system.

Climate change also makes poverty worse, especially for vulnerable communities. Strong storms, floods, and changing weather can destroy homes, farms, and jobs. Still, citizens can take part in solutions. The United States Environmental Protection Agency suggests that people can join community efforts like tree planting, creating green spaces, and taking part in local planning to reduce climate risks (EPA, 2026). These actions help protect both the environment and people’s livelihoods. In addition, cities and communities play an important role in solving climate problems. The United Nations Development Programme states that using clean energy, improving infrastructure, and planning sustainable cities can reduce harm to the environment while also creating jobs (UNDP, 2024). By getting involved in these efforts, citizens help build stronger communities that can better handle climate challenges.

In the end, these global problems may be complex, but they are not impossible to address. Through simple choices like buying local, supporting fair businesses, and joining community efforts, ordinary people can help create real change. Step by step, these actions can help break the cycle of poverty and build a more fair and sustainable future.
