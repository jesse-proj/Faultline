The Trap of Raw Resource Extraction: Resource Rich, but Economically Disadvantaged

By Aimi Omori | March 2026

Nickel is a highly valued and versatile element. It has been used in energy storage technologies for years, primarily due to its ability to improve storage capacity at a low cost. This capability was instrumental in miniaturizing batteries and enabling the portable electronic devices that we use every single day. It is also a key material in the cathodes of lithium-ion batteries used for electric vehicles (EVs). It is estimated that the demand for nickel will grow by as much as 40% by 2030, as bans on internal combustion engine vehicles approach in many markets (IEF, 2024).

However, why is the Philippines still poor, despite being a major contributor to the nickel market and the second-largest producer of nickel ore globally?

According to Ventura (2025), citing findings gathered by Global Finance Magazine, the Philippines ranked 72nd as the "poorest country in the world in 2025 based on GDP (PPP) per capita out of 193 countries." Furthermore, it is performing poorly compared to its Southeast Asian peers. While it is not ranking at the very bottom, it often ranks 5th or 6th as the poorest country in the region by GDP per capita.

It is not that the country is inherently poor; rather, it suffers from poor management and systemic issues. Instead of processing these resources domestically into high-value heavy machinery or electronics, which could support the local industry, they export raw ore at low prices.

"By exporting raw materials, the wealth generated by 'value-adding' manufacturing stays in wealthy nations, leaving the source country with the environmental costs but none of the industrial profit."

The Philippines is one example among many in the Global South struggling to prosper due to a heavy dependence on raw exports. It is ironic that even with its abundant natural riches, the Philippines is in a far worse economic situation than other countries that are far less endowed (Habito, 2022).

Simply relying on natural resources is not enough. What is valued today is the finished product manufactured from these materials because that is what consumers actually buy; even the goods imported into the country hold more value than the raw materials that are exported.

In spite of this, many countries, including the Philippines, face "tariff escalation," wherein processed or finished goods are taxed more heavily than the raw materials used to make them (UNCTAD, 2025).

Even though raw materials are essentially worthless unless processed, the system itself hinders the country from developing. This is also why the Philippines—a resource-rich country—chooses to sell raw materials: it is simply cheaper.

The country fails to fully maximize the value of its resources due to various barriers in the modern world that prevent it from growing. This points to a larger systemic issue and underscores the need for reforms in global trade, which currently heavily favors wealthier nations.

Labor Exploitation and Wealth Gap

By Manoah Linuel Dane | March 2026

Globalization has allowed the world to connect in many different aspects of life. Despite such a positive description, it is unfortunate that the concept has also brought forth cases of exploitation and inequality.

One crucial trend related to this inequality is found in the tech industry, specifically regarding how electronics manufacturers opt for cheaper labor costs in foreign countries. The issue of phone manufacturing in Vietnam serves as a prime example of this in the contemporary world.

Vietnam offers low labor costs, prompting electronics manufacturers to focus their product assembly within the country. Major production regions include the northern provinces of Bac Ninh, Thai Nguyen, and Hai Phong, along with the southern provinces of Ho Chi Minh City (HCMC) and Binh Duong (Center of Development and Integration, 2019).

"It is evident that despite the innovation globalization brings to the tech industry, it is overshadowed by the human cost it takes to achieve."

Samsung and Panasonic maintain a major presence across these two regions, and there are more than 500 electronics companies operating in the northern region alone (Center of Development and Integration, 2019).

Laborers in Vietnam are often subjected to simple yet repetitive and demanding tasks that extend over long hours. Yet, the government continues to pursue more complex production opportunities. Advanced consumer goods have become a priority, driven by the goal of supposed economic development (Sheldon and Kwon, 2023).

In reality, these workers are not given the proper opportunities to meet the demands placed upon them. Employers and the government invest very little in meaningful vocational education and training (VET) (Sheldon and Kwon, 2023). Furthermore, the workers' well-being is severely compromised by health issues, dangerous working conditions, discrimination, extensive hours, and low wages (Labour Law Compliance and the Role of Labour Inspection in Viet Nam's Electronics Sector, n.d.).

A closer look into the work environment of these laborers raises numerous concerns. Many workers remain completely unaware of their exposure to hazards such as magnetic radiation, harmful chemicals, and toxic fumes. Specific health issues primarily include headaches, backaches, and nausea, alongside reported cases of rashes and even fainting (Center of Development and Integration, 2019).

Exploitation, such as that seen within the Vietnamese electronics industry, is simply not a valid price to pay for better technology.

The Global Environment Crisis and Poverty

By Leonard Reforma | March 2026

Developing nations face a continuous cycle of disaster recovery, severely stunting long-term economic growth. A staggering new report reveals the devastating financial and human cost of the climate crisis, proving that low-income nations are paying the highest price for global emissions.

According to the Germanwatch Climate Risk Index 2026, extreme weather events caused over $4.5 trillion in cumulative economic losses globally between 1995 and 2024. However, the burden of these staggering losses is not shared equally across the globe.

The data highlights a glaring disparity: eight of the ten countries most affected by climate disasters in 2024 fall into the low- or lower-middle-income brackets. Storms accounted for a massive all-time high of 58% of total economic damages, followed closely by catastrophic, widespread flooding that decimated local agriculture and housing.

"These vulnerable nations are caught in a relentless 'poverty trap,' entirely unable to financially recover from one climate disaster before the next one strikes."

This relentless and constant cycle makes long-term economic development almost impossible. When a severe typhoon or multi-week flood destroys regional infrastructure, cash crops, and homes, developing nations are forced to divert their scarce sovereign resources. Funding is pulled away from education, healthcare, and poverty reduction, and poured instead into immediate emergency recovery and debris removal projects.

The Germanwatch findings underscore the urgent need for robust international climate finance mechanisms. Without substantial and immediate support from high-emitting, wealthier nations—specifically through functional Loss and Damage funds—the world's poorest populations will continue to bear the crushing weight of a climate crisis they did little to create.

How Drought and Conflict are Starving Millions in Somalia

Somalia, the easternmost country in continental Africa, is facing a devastating humanitarian crisis. Poverty, recurrent droughts, and ongoing conflict are threatening to push 4.4 million people into severe hunger by April 2025. Currently, around 3.4 million Somalis are already experiencing starvation at a crisis level, eroding the fragile livelihoods of the nation's vulnerable populations.

The intersection of extreme weather and poverty across the nation is taking an economic toll on local communities. Those most affected include:

  • Small-scale farmers: Suffering from low agricultural yields and depleted food stocks due to erratic rainfall.
  • Pastoralists: Who have lost their livestock and, consequently, their main source of income.
  • Internally displaced persons (IDPs).

These compounding effects have caused economic shocks and triggered skyrocketing food prices, making basic survival needs unaffordable for millions. As families plunge deeper into poverty, the nutritional impact remains catastrophic. An estimated 1.7 million children under five are expected to face acute malnutrition by the end of 2025.

Despite the escalating need to protect these impoverished communities, humanitarian efforts are being bottlenecked and crippled by severe financial shortfalls. The 2025 Somalia Humanitarian Need and Response Plan is currently only 12.4% funded, forcing aid agencies to cut life-saving programs just as the crisis deepens. Without immediate and fully funded interventions to support agricultural resilience, provide cash assistance, and deliver emergency relief, Somalia risks a return to the catastrophic famine conditions seen in 2022.

The Disproportionate Burden: Climate Change and Poverty in the Philippines

Based on the 2026 OECD Economic Survey of the Philippines, climate change is a compounding economic burden for the country's most vulnerable populations. The report, "Confronting Climate Changes," highlights how environmental shocks and the transition to a greener economy intersect with poverty in several critical ways:

  • Agricultural Vulnerability: The agricultural sector in the Philippines is a primary source of income and livelihood for many lower-income Filipinos, yet it is highly susceptible to climate extremes. Droughts, severe heat, and typhoons threaten crop yields and food security, directly impacting the economic stability of farming communities.
  • The Protection Gap for the Poor: When weather disasters strike, lower-income segments of the population are the most exposed to catastrophic losses but are the least protected. A severe lack of financial inclusion, insufficient income to afford insurance premiums, and financial illiteracy prevent poorer classes from accessing property or disaster insurance. This forces vulnerable families to bear the full financial brunt of climate-related damages, pushing them deeper into poverty.
  • Funding for Vulnerable Communities: The country is actively working to establish a market-based emissions trading system (ETS) under House Bill No. 11375. If successful, this could direct public revenues generated from selling carbon credits explicitly toward impoverished communities—funding local green investments and building climate resilience where it is needed most.

Ultimately, the report concludes that while the Philippines must act aggressively to tackle climate change, successful policies must integrate robust financial protections and safety nets to prevent the poorest citizens from being left behind.

Breaking the Cycle: How Ordinary People Can Help Fight Global Inequality

By Eric Jon Gamalong | March 2026

In today's global economy, many developing countries face systemic problems that keep their populations stuck in poverty. These include relying too much on exporting raw materials, unfair treatment of workers, and the growing effects of climate change. While these issues may seem too massive to solve, ordinary citizens can still make a real difference through their everyday choices and actions.

One major problem is the trap of raw resource extraction. Many countries sell raw materials instead of turning them into finished products, which limits their income and growth. One practical way people can help is by supporting locally made goods. When consumers choose local products, they help strengthen domestic industries and reduce the need to rely on exporting raw materials. According to the Philippine News Agency, supporting local production can make the economy much stronger, especially during global crises (Zaldarriaga, 2025). Buying local also encourages businesses to invest in better technology and create higher-value products. Over time, this generates jobs, supports small businesses, and builds a more stable economy.

"Through both personal choices and group action, citizens can help create a fairer and more equitable system."

Another issue is labor exploitation and the growing gap between the rich and the poor. Many workers are paid incredibly low wages while large companies siphon most of the profit. Citizens can help by becoming more mindful of what they buy. Every single purchase supports how a product was made and how workers were treated. By consistently choosing companies that provide fair wages and safe working conditions, people can actively push businesses to improve their practices (Sustainability Directory, n.d.).

At the same time, collective action remains important. Supporting unions, cooperatives, and worker rights groups helps give workers a stronger voice. The International Labour Organization emphasizes that fair wages and collective bargaining are key to ensuring workers are treated with respect and compensated properly (International Labour Organization, n.d.).

Climate change also continually worsens poverty, particularly for vulnerable communities. Strong storms, floods, and shifting weather patterns can decimate homes, farms, and jobs. Yet, citizens can take part in the solutions. The United States Environmental Protection Agency suggests that individuals can join community efforts like tree planting, creating green spaces, and participating in local planning to mitigate climate risks (EPA, 2026). These actions protect both the environment and people's livelihoods.

In addition, sustainable cities and communities play an indispensable role in solving climate problems. The United Nations Development Programme states that adopting clean energy, strengthening infrastructure, and planning sustainable cities can significantly reduce environmental harm while synchronously creating new green jobs (UNDP, 2024). By getting involved in grassroots efforts, citizens enable stronger communities that are better equipped to handle climate challenges.

Ultimately, these global problems may be deeply complex, but they are not impossible to address. Through simple choices like buying local, supporting fair businesses, and joining community efforts, ordinary people have the power to enact real change. Step by step, proactive actions can help break the cycle of poverty and build a more fair and sustainable future for all.
